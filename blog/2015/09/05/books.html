At a glance:

My ten recommended books for applied statistics and data science. Then 12 more! Plus two I don't like, and why.

These days if you want to learn applied statistics and data science tools, you have amazing options in the form of blogs, (Q&A sites](http://stats.stackexchange.com/), and massive open online courses and videos on You Tube. Wikipedia is an amazing resource on statistics.

I use all those things to learn new techniques and understand old ones better, but I also love reading books. No, I’m not one of those sentimental people who go on about the texture of paper; while I do like the look and feel of a “real” book and love having them around, it’s a pain the way the take up space, and the big majority of books I buy these days are on an e-reader. So when I say I like “books”, it’s something the depth and the focus that comes with a good book going deeply into a matter.

My ten favourite applied statistics and data science books

Here’s my favourite books on the topic of this blog. Necessarily it’s a personal and idiosyncratic list. I haven’t read all the classics in the field and I certainly don’t have time to read all the new things that come out. But I read a fair number of books, including quite a few that I think are boring, or sub-standard, or unnecessary. This list is of books that I come back to time and time again; and / or that made a huge impact on me when I read them at the right time.

McCullagh and Nelder, Generalized Linear Models. What an amazing classic! What a triumph of twentieth century statistics, which I think can really be seen as culminating in the Generalized Linear Model (GLM, bringing together things as diverse as the Chi Square test for independence in cross-tabs, survival analysis, Poisson, logistic and classical regression, and so much more. GLMs are everwhere, and they should probably be more places than they are. As Eric D. Brown relates an un-named computer science expert tweeted about by Robin Hanson::

“Most firms that thinks they want advanced AI/ML really just need linear regression on cleaned-up data”.

Or as Hadley Wickham pointed out on Quora, if he had pick just one topic in statistics that every data scientist should know, it is linear models.

Harrell, Regression Modeling Strategies.

Efron and Hastie, Computer-Age Statistical Inference: Algorithms, Evidence, and Data Science.

Modern Statistics for the Social and Behavioural Sciences Modern Applied Statistics with S Regression Modelling Strategies Applied Predictive Modelling Advanced R ggplot2: Elegant Graphics for Data Analysis Visual Display of Quantitative Information and its sequels Visualizing Data and The Elements of Graphic Data Cleveland’s books
